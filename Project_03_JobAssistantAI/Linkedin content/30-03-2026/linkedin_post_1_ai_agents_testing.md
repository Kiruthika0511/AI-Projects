# LinkedIn Post 1 — Topic: AI Agents for Autonomous Test Execution

---

I watched a QA team of 3 go from 200 test cases to 900 in 6 weeks — without hiring anyone.

They did not use a test management tool upgrade. They did not work weekends.

They gave an AI agent the spec and told it to write the tests.

---

The agent was not magic. It read the requirements, generated test scenarios, flagged ambiguous acceptance criteria, and wrote Playwright scripts. Every output was reviewable. Every run was logged.

But here is the part that stuck with me.

The team lead told me: "Our job changed. We stopped writing tests and started reviewing intent." That shift — from author to editor — is not a small one. It changes what you need to know, how you spend your time, and what seniority means in QA.

The agents are not replacing QA engineers. They are replacing the part of QA that nobody wanted to do anyway: the repetitive first-draft scaffolding. What they cannot replace is judgment. Knowing which test matters. Knowing when a pass is hiding a real bug.

That is the skill that survives every wave of automation — and the one most teams are still not deliberately building.

The tools are not the threat. The failure to adapt is.
